If you are reading this because someone you care about has died or can no longer
manage their own affairs, we are sorry. Whatever you are feeling right now is
allowed. This page is written for you — the person who opens the box, not the one
who built it — and its only job is to help you find your footing.

Someone cared enough to gather this information so that you would not have to
piece it together alone. None of it has to be understood today, and none of it
has to be done perfectly.

## First, you can slow down

Very little here needs to happen in the next hour. It is a common instinct to
feel you must act fast, but most of what a box holds can wait until you have
rested and have someone beside you. Take the time you need.

- **You do not have to do this alone.** Ask a trusted friend or family member to
  sit with you while you go through the box, even just to read things aloud or
  take notes.
- **You do not have to finish in one sitting.** Do a little, set it down, and
  come back. Nothing in the box expires because you closed it for the day.
- **You do not have to be an expert.** This box points you toward the people and
  documents you will need. It does not expect you to already know how any of it
  works.

If someone is in immediate danger, that is the exception — call emergency
services or a medical professional first, and come back to the box afterward.

## Start with the master note

Most boxes open with a single overview — sometimes called a *master note* or a
*letter of instruction*. It is a plain-language summary of where everything is
and who to contact. If you find one, read it first. It is meant to be your map,
and it will usually tell you what the rest of the box contains and in what order
the owner hoped you would approach it.

If there is no single note, that is all right. Look for whatever is most general
— a contents list, an index, a first envelope marked to be opened first — and
begin there.

## A few gentle first steps

When you are ready, these are the things that are usually worth doing early. Take
them in whatever order makes sense for your situation, and skip anything that does
not apply.

1. **Find the people to notify.** The box should name the first people and
   organizations to contact — close family, a named executor or representative,
   an employer, and any professional who was already helping, such as an
   attorney, accountant, or financial advisor. Reaching one of these people
   early often means you do not have to carry the next steps by yourself.

2. **Locate the key documents.** Note where the important papers are kept, rather
   than trying to act on them right away — things like a will, a power of
   attorney, insurance policies, or identification. You are making a map of what
   exists, not making decisions yet.

3. **Look for wishes about care and arrangements.** Some boxes include notes
   about medical directives, or preferences for a funeral or memorial. If a
   time-sensitive decision is in front of you, these are the pages to check for
   guidance on what the person would have wanted — and share what you find with
   the medical team or care providers, who can tell you how it applies.

4. **Understand how to reach the essentials.** The box may explain how a trusted
   person can access a phone, email, or password manager. These often unlock
   everything else, so keep anything sensitive secure and share it only with the
   people who genuinely need it.

5. **Keep a simple record as you go.** Jot down who you have contacted and what
   you have found. A short running list saves you from retracing your steps and
   makes it easier to hand things off or ask for help.

## The kinds of things a box may cover

Beyond the first steps, a fuller box may point you toward several areas of the
person's life. You will not need all of them, and you can approach them one at a
time:

- **Legal** — a will, powers of attorney, and the name of anyone appointed to
  settle the estate.
- **Financial** — bank, retirement, and investment accounts, along with any
  insurance, benefits, or outstanding bills.
- **Digital** — email, phone, password manager, and important online accounts.
- **Funeral and personal wishes** — instructions or preferences the person wanted
  honored, and messages they may have left behind.

As this guide grows, each of these areas will have its own section written for
you. For now, work from what the box itself contains, and lean on the master note
to tell you where each of these lives.

## When to bring in a professional

Some parts of settling a person's affairs are genuinely legal or financial work,
and you are not expected to handle them alone. If the person named someone to act
on their behalf — often called an *executor* — that role can carry real
responsibilities, and the exact rules depend on where the person lived.

You may want to speak with a professional when:

- you have been named to settle an estate and are not sure what that involves;
- there is a will, property, or debts that need to be formally handled;
- you are asked to make legal or medical decisions on someone's behalf; or
- anything in the box is unclear and the stakes feel high.

OpenNOK explains how a box is organized so you can find your way through it. It is
not legal advice, and the rules for wills, estates, and directives differ from one
place to another. A qualified attorney or advisor can tell you what applies to
your situation — and reaching out to one is a normal, sensible step, not a sign
that anything has gone wrong.

The [glossary](/glossary) explains terms like *executor* in plain language if you
come across a word the box assumes you already know.

## Be kind to yourself

You have been handed something hard, and you are doing it while carrying a loss.
Go at the pace grief allows. The box will keep. Do what you can today, ask for
help where you can, and trust that a partial start is still a real one.

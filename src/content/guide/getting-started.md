A full next-of-kin box takes time to assemble, but a useful one does not. If you
only have an afternoon, this is the shortest path to a box your loved ones could
actually use — the small set of things that carry the most weight. Start small
and add depth later; even a partial box is far better than none.

## The minimum-viable box

The minimum-viable box is a single, findable place — a folder, an envelope, a
sealed document, or a note you keep updated — that answers one question for the
people you trust: where is everything, and how do they reach it? Everything
below builds on that.

## Do these first

Work through these in order. Each step stands on its own, so stop whenever you
run out of time and you will still have left something useful behind.

1. **Write down where everything is.** Start with a single master note —
   sometimes called a *letter of instruction* — that says where your important
   documents, accounts, and belongings live. This one note is the most valuable
   thing in the box: it turns a scavenger hunt into a short read, and it does
   not need to be complete to be useful.

2. **List the people to notify.** Name the handful of people and organizations
   someone should contact first: close family, your employer, anyone who
   depends on you, and any professional who already helps you — an attorney,
   accountant, or financial advisor. Note names and how to reach them.

3. **Explain how to get in.** Record how a trusted person can reach your
   essentials — your phone, your email, and, most importantly, your password
   manager if you use one. Access to those usually unlocks everything else. Keep
   this part secured, and tell only the people who need it where to find it.

4. **Inventory the money and coverage.** Note where your accounts are — banks,
   retirement or investment accounts, and any insurance or benefits — without
   listing full numbers or passwords in plain sight. The goal is a map, not a
   vault: enough for someone to know what exists and who to call.

5. **Point to your legal directives.** If you have a will, a power of attorney,
   or a healthcare or medical directive, note where the signed originals are
   kept and who your named contacts are. If you do not have these yet, that is
   fine — this guide is not the place to write them, and the rules differ from
   one place to another. A qualified professional can help you put them in place.

## The one-page checklist

Here is the whole minimum-viable box as a checklist. If every line has an answer
someone could find, you have a working box.

- A master note saying where everything is
- The first people and organizations to notify
- How to reach your phone, email, and password manager
- A list of your accounts, insurance, and benefits
- Where your will and directives are kept, if you have them

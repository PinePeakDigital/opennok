If you only ever finish one part of your box, make it this one. A master index is
a single overview — sometimes called a *master note*, a *letter of instruction*,
or simply *where everything is* — that tells the people you trust what exists and
where to find it. It is the map to everything else.

Every other section of this guide describes something you might gather: accounts,
documents, wishes, ways to get in touch. The master index is the page that points
to all of it. That is why it carries more weight than any single item it lists.

## Why it's the keystone

Without a master index, the people you leave in charge are left doing detective
work at the worst possible time — opening drawers, guessing at passwords, calling
around to find out whether a policy even exists. With one, that same search
becomes a short read.

A good master index does three things:

- **It turns a scavenger hunt into a short read.** Instead of piecing your life
  together from fragments, the finder starts with one page that tells them what
  to look for and where each thing lives.
- **It works even when nothing else is finished.** A box does not have to be
  complete to be useful. If your index simply says "the will is at the lawyer's
  office" and "the accounts are listed in the blue folder," it has already done
  most of its job.
- **It shows the order to work in.** By listing what matters first, the index
  quietly tells the finder where to begin — which is exactly what a person facing
  a loss or an emergency needs.

This is the page the [person who finds your box](/guide/for-the-finder) is told to
read first. Everything you write here is written for them.

## A map, not a vault

The single most important rule for a master index: it is a **map, not a vault.**
Its job is to point to where things are, not to hold the secrets themselves.

Write down *where* your accounts are and *who* to call — not full account numbers,
PINs, or passwords in plain text. If your index is lost, copied, or seen by the
wrong person, a map is a mild inconvenience; a vault is a serious problem.

For the things that genuinely unlock everything else — your phone, your email, and
your password manager most of all — record *how* a trusted person can reach them
and *where* that access is kept secured, rather than writing the secrets on the
page. Keep the sensitive part protected, and tell only the people who truly need
it where to find it. If a trusted person can get into your password manager, they
can usually reach everything else without you ever having to list it here.

## Build your master index

Below is a fillable template. Work through the sections that apply to you and
skip the ones that do not — a partial index is still far better than none. You
can fill it in on paper, in a document, or by hand on a printout of this page;
whatever you will actually keep up to date is the right format.

For each prompt, write *where the thing is* or *who to contact* — a location, a
folder, a person, a phone number. Resist the urge to write the secrets
themselves. If you are not sure a line applies to you, leave it blank.

Start by orienting the finder to the box itself.

  representative):

- Anyone who depends on you, or who cares for someone you are responsible for:

## Keep it current

A master index is only as good as its last update. Set a gentle reminder — once a
year, or whenever something big changes, like a move, a new account, or a change
in who you trust — to read it through and fix anything out of date. The "last
reviewed" date at the bottom of this page is there for the same reason: it tells a
reader how fresh the information is.

Then keep the index somewhere the right people can actually find it, and make sure
at least one person you trust knows it exists. An index no one can find helps no
one.

## Where to go next

When your master index points to something you have not gathered yet, that is your
cue for what to build next. The [full guide](/guide) covers each area — accounts,
documents, digital access, and wishes — in its own section, and the
[Quick start](/guide/getting-started) is the shortest path if you are just
beginning. If a word here is unfamiliar, the [glossary](/glossary) keeps
plain-language definitions in one place.

There is no rush, and it does not have to be perfect. The best index is the one
that exists — start with whatever you can answer today, and add to it over time.
